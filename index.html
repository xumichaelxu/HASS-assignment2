Example 1 - Happy Planet Index

The infographic data illustrates happy planet index in some countries involves various factors such as happiness, sunlight, GDP, fatness, murder, and population density rankings for different countries.

Why

The amount and complexity information presented in this single graphic may potentially create confusion and misinterpretation among readers. The legend accompanying the infographic lacks consistency in specifying the range of maximum or minimum measurement. For instance, the United Kingdom is shown with a population density slightly above 15 (as indicated on the left), while the Netherlands has a population density of over 1000 (as indicated on the right). It is unclear which measurement scale should be followed, potentially leading to further misinterpretation of the data as either rankings or actual values. Another example, Singapore is depicted with its GDP and population density are represented with the same color on the graph. This could lead viewers to have a swapped interpretation at a glance between population density and the GDP. Furthermore, the continuous wave lines connecting to each country may lead to a different perspective on points or measurements, making it difficult to interpret the information accurately.

Source

Conclusion

To address these issues and improve the design, it is recommended to revise the legend to clearly indicate whether the numbers represent rankings or actual values. Furthermore, using contrast colour such as population density versus GDP , will enable clearer category. Additionally, the use of distinct colors and clear labeling for each data point will help avoid confusion and facilitate accurate interpretation. Finally, reevaluating the graphical elements, such as the wave lines with additional dot points, to ensure clearer measurement will be beneficial. These modifications will result in a clearer and more user-friendly infographic for readers to understand and interpret the Happy Planet Index data effectively.

Example 2 - Layout

The pie chart illustrates the forecast of biggest future changes in the workplace

Why

The name "forecast" may raise questions regarding the reliability of the measurement model. The chart includes three categories: "employees paid based on output," "salary flexibility," and "modified working desk with treadmill." However, the comparison lacks relevance in terms of employee work preferences, employer payment methods, and office desk design. One glaring mistake in the infographic is the total percentages of the pie chart, which exceeds 100% and adds up to 188%. Additionally, the choice of icons for each category appears to be inaccurate. For example, both flexibility over salaries and the treadmill desk are represented by the same exercise-related icons.

Source

Conclusion

In terms of title, perhaps it is better if we call it as "Workplace Preferences and Benefits" rather than "Biggest Future Changes in Workplace". The word forecast here seems too heavy as there is no explanation numbers here. The category seems too long and can be simplify by providing "Performance Based Compensation", "Flexibility Structures", and "Workspace Innovation". It is definitely has to change the percentage to maintain the accuracy, or perhaps to change the chart illustration. Finally, it is important to avoid icons confusion that may lead misinterpretation.
